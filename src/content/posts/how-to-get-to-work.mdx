## The Motivating Problem

Let's consider the age-old problem of deciding which route to take to work. Suppose you have a new job that requires
commuting to a location you've never travelled to before. There are naturally a range of options, and
we'll assume that you know all of the possible routes but don't have Waze or any other such tools available to tell you
which one would be the fastest on any given day.

To simplify the modeling problem, let's assume that every route is fully independent of the others, so taking one route
doesn't give you any information about another.

Whenever you take a given route to work, you obtain some amount of **reward**. This "reward" will be defined 
differently for different people, but it is essentially a number that indicates how much you liked the commute. 
For some, the rewrad of a route will simply be the inverse of how long the commute took. 
For others, it might depend on the commute length and time spent in traffic; some people prefer a slightly
longer commute by a more circuitous route if it means that they're not sitting in gridlock the entire time. In any case
we will assume that there exists some personalized, mathematical function, a *reward function*, capable of describing 
how much you liked a given commute.

In decision-theory research, we typically assume that people (and robots, and AIs) are trying to maximize their 
**lifetime reward**. Their lifetime reward is the total amount of reward they accumulate over every decision they
ever make. This would mean that, ideally, a person would identify the best route (for *them*, based on *their*
personal reward function).

So, the question becomes: **what is the most intelligent way for a person to decide how to get to work each day?**

Our assumptions from above make it so that this can be modelled as a very specific problem type that has been widely studied
in academic literature: the *multi-armed bandit problem*. Essentially, we call the different routes to work different
*arms*, and we refer to picking a particular route one day as "pulling an arm". Many other problems can be modelled this
way, ranging from deciding which assets to invest in to determining which new experimental drug is most effective for 
treating a particular ailment.

A core aspect of this type multi-armed bandit problems is that there is some amount of randomness in the
outcome every time you pull a particular arm. For example, traffic tends to vary randomly day-to-day, which means
that a given route may be quick one day but slow the next. Some arms may be more random than others, for example
if one route is usually quite quick but is also more prone to delays caused by traffic accidents.

## A Straightforward Approach

So, how does an intelligent, rational agent decide how to get to work each day? Let's start by considering a simple
strategy. Suppose that you try every route once. And then, because you might have been lucky (or unlucky) the first
time you tried a route, you try every route again. And to build your confidence further, you try each of them 
a few more times. Finally, you determine which route tends to give you the most reward, on average, and from then 
on you always take that particular "best" route. We call this strategy "explore-then-commit", or **ETC**.

This can actually be a very effective strategy, but it introduces a new question: *how many times should you test 
each route before committing to one of them?* The answer to this question turns out to depend on a variety of factors,
such as:
- How "random" (variable) is the reward obtained from each route?
- How many different routes are there?
- How long do you expect to be working at this particular company/location?

To see why these matter, let's suppose there are 10 different ways to get to work and you only expect to be commuting
to this location for the next 2 months. With only ~50 work days at this location, testing each route 5 times would mean
that you would only finally take advantage of knowing the "best" route to work on your last day!

On the other hand, suppose you expect to work at this location for the next 30 years. In that case, testing each route only
5 times might be far from enough. Suppose that you were unlucky in 3-4 of your test runs for a particular route which,
in reality, was the best one for you based on your "reward function". This could cause you to commit to a different route, 
a *sub-optimal route*, at the end of your first 2 months, which you would end up taking every day for the next 358 months.

Whenever you take a sub-optimal route to work, you incur some amount of **regret**. Regret is essentially the opposite of 
reward. It represents *how much extra reward you could have obtained by taking a different route* (or "pulling a different 
arm"). **Importantly**, the decision-theory definition of regret differs from the typical mainstream definition. Typically, a
person regrets a decision only if they know (or at least suspect) that it was sub-optimal (i.e., not as good as it could have 
been). In decision-theory, we often consider the case where a person/"agent" doesn't actually know that their decision was 
wrong. We call this the "actual regret" incurred by the agent, to distinguish it from "known", *observed* regret.

Maximizing lifetime reward is mathematically equivalent to minimizing lifetime regret, trying to do one is exactly the same
as trying to do the other. It is generally easier to work with lifetime regret, as it has the nice property that it can never
be negative, and the "best" possible value is 0. With lifetime reward, it's not obvious if a given value is "good" or not 
relative to the other choices a person could have made. However, an agent with no lifetime regret means that it never made
a bad decision in its whole life.

The explore-then-commit strategy can often do a good job of maximizing reward (i.e., minimizing regret), at least when it is
tuned properly. The key facet of ETC is that you incur a lot of regret during the "explore" phase, but the more you explore,
the less regret you incur during the "commit" phase. So, "tuning it" means choosing the length of the exploration period 
such that it's not so long that you spend a large chunk of your career incurring high exploration regret, but also not so 
short that you commit too early to the wrong choice and spend the majority of your career paying for your rashness.

## Issues with Explore-Then-Commit

The ETC algorithm was one of the earliest developed for the multi-armed bandit (MAB) problem, and it can be surprisingly
effective given its simplicity. However, it has many notable flaws. Most critically, it does a poor job of *allocating arm 
pulls* (called "trials") *where they are needed most*.

For example, suppose there are two "good" routes to work, and eight "bad" ones, but one of the good routes is at least
marginally better than the other. You probably don't need to test out the bad routes more than a couple of times to 
establish that they can't compete with the two good routes, but it might take many trials to compare the two good routes
before you can tell which one of them the best. This reflects the intuition that you should assign more exploratory trials
to the routes which are "promising" contenders, and fewer to those that are obviously inferior. This reflects a trial 
allocation that is inversely proportional to the perceived *gap* between a given arm and whichever one appears to be the best.

Similarly, the more "random" routes require more tests to detemrine how good they actually are. It is quick
to identify the average reward of a stable, consistent route. It may require many trials, however, to determine the average
reward of a highly variable one. It becomes even more complicated when we consider that *it takes a few trials to even
determine how random/variable a given route is.* 

These considerations suggest that it is possible to design a much better algorithm than ETC, which ignores the factors above
and assigns an equal number of exploratory trials to all contenders. This algorithmic regret (sub-optimality) means that using ETC
generally causes an agent to incur more "regret" than another one would. While this algorithmic regret may not always be a 
major concern (for example, when using it to choose your morning commute each day), in other applications like medical trials
these "regrets" may represent things like poor health outcomes that we are strongly motivated to avoid. This has motivated
the development of significantly more capable, but generally more complicated, algorithms for making good decisions in these
types of contexts. We call these algorithms "policies", which is a general term for an algorithm that tells an agent what 
to do.
