The single most useful thing a deployed model can learn is *when to keep its mouth shut*. A classifier that is right 92% of the time sounds fine until you learn that the 8% it gets wrong are scattered through high-stakes decisions. **Selective prediction** changes the contract: the model may either answer or **abstain**, and we judge it only on the questions it chose to answer.<Sidenote n="1">This is exactly the posture behind uncertainty-aware deployment: route the confident cases to automation and the uncertain ones to a human.</Sidenote>

## The coverage–accuracy trade-off

Suppose every prediction comes with an uncertainty score $u_i \in [0, 1]$. We pick a threshold $\tau$ and answer only when $u_i \le \tau$. Two quantities then matter. **Coverage** is the fraction of inputs we actually answer:

$$
\mathrm{coverage}(\tau) = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} \mathbb{1}\!\left[u_i \le \tau\right]
$$

and **selective accuracy** is how often we are right *on the answered subset*:

$$
\mathrm{acc}(\tau) = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N} \mathbb{1}\!\left[u_i \le \tau\right]\,\mathbb{1}\!\left[\hat{y}_i = y_i\right]}{\sum_{i=1}^{N} \mathbb{1}\!\left[u_i \le \tau\right]}.
$$

As $\tau \to 0$ we answer almost nothing but are nearly always right; as $\tau \to 1$ we answer everything and collapse back to ordinary accuracy. The art is choosing where on that curve to sit.

## Computing it

The bookkeeping is a few lines:

```python
import numpy as np

def selective_metrics(uncertainty, correct, tau):
    """Coverage and selective accuracy at threshold tau."""
    answered = uncertainty <= tau
    coverage = answered.mean()
    sel_acc = correct[answered].mean() if answered.any() else float("nan")
    return coverage, sel_acc

# Sweep tau to trace the full risk–coverage curve.
taus = np.linspace(0.02, 1.0, 50)
curve = [selective_metrics(u, correct, t) for t in taus]
```

The payoff: instead of reporting a single accuracy number, you report a *curve*, and you get to choose the operating point that matches the cost of being wrong. That choice — not the last decimal of benchmark accuracy — is usually what determines whether a model is safe to ship.
